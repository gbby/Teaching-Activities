# Professional Resources

## Jobs and Internships
If you plan to go on the data job market, there are a bunch of things you should be aware of. Pay attention to the language that is used in job ads. What skills are they asking for? What pay are they offering? The data job market is tough. You will compete against people with fake credentials, more experience, better grades, more advanced skills... the best thing you can do is try to stand out. If you don't have the requisite experience, it doesn't hurt to contact the recruiter or company and make yourself available - maybe there is a more junior position available! Don't lie about your professional experience, but you shouldn't discount the work you've done in courses, and you should be developing a portfolio that helps you stand out. More on that below.

## Portfolio Development
"When hiring analysts, there is one thing that catches my attention every time I see it on a resume: A personal blog of analysis on public data. It’s not that the existence of a blog is a signal; it’s that blogs give you an immediate glimpse of the type of analyst their author is. Are they drawn to machine learning or statistical projects? Do they focus on data collection and cleanup? Are they curious problem solvers, and see data as an interrogative means to an inquisitive end? Blogs also expose how clever they are as an analyst, how crisp they are as a communicator, and a bit about their style and personality along the way... Like design portfolios, an analytical portfolio would be a short exhibition of professional work. It would walk through a few projects the analyst completed, and share the context behind different problems, how they solved them, and the impact of their efforts. It could explain their thought process and their analytical wanderings. They could include partially redacted charts, slides, and dashboards that they built." 
- Benn Stancil, Chief Analytics Officer at Mode https://benn.substack.com/p/analysts-should-have-portfolios 

- This might be very basic to those of you who have web or graphic design experience, or who already have your own websites, but it's not designed for you. This tutorial is really designed for any of you who doesn't have a clue how to start. It could be an entire course, in fact, so compressing it into a short tutorial won't get you far, but I want to show you how easy it is to get up and running with a very simple portfolio.

### Getting Started
- How to start: Take stock of your projects. You'll want to highlight the things you're good at and what differentiates you. Ask yourself: what are your strengths? What are your technical skills?
- One good starting point is to look at how famous visualization people share their work https://lenagroeger.com/
- Or look to students in data visualization courses to see how they share their work https://aemichalowski.github.io/Data-Visualization-Portfolio/
- What to include in a project-based portfolio: course projects; internship work; software dev; concepts in development
- Alternatively, you can include functional sections: writing examples; wireframing; teaching; UX/UI; project mgmt; photography and video; digital dev; interactives; game design; interaction design.
- You should consider including unfinished stuff like sketches, wireframes, and even moodboards.
- Wireframe/mock-up the entire portfolio with Google Draw or any of the tools listed here https://alternativeto.net/software/balsamiq-mockups/. Google Draw is nice because it is integrated in Drive, and you can easily share your portfolio mock-up with peers to get feedback.

### Squarespace Portfolios
- Ok... let's consider what it would take to build a simple portfolio with squarespace. I promise you that I'm not getting paid like all those goofball youtubers and podcasters!
- Sign up for a Squarespace account by clicking on "Get Started" at the top of https://www.squarespace.com/
- There are lots of good youtube videos that show how to set up Squarespace portfolio websites: https://www.youtube.com/results?search_query=squarespace+portfolio
- This set of simple instructions will walk you through creating a simple portfolio: https://www.squarespace.com/how-to/make-a-portfolio
- This instructional is, in my opinion, the most comprehensive and beginner-friendly for students: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u-DnVBrzeLs
- Regarding which template you should use, I'd go with Nevin or Beaumont if you want a nice portfolio landing page.
- To add a specific Portfolio page to your site:
  - In the Home Menu, click Pages, then click + next to the navigation section where you want to add the page.
  - In the menu, click Portfolio and choose a layout. You can change this later.
  - Enter a page title and press Enter.
  - Click Edit, then click the pencil icon to style the section.
  - Click + to add a sub-page or click an existing sub-page, then click Edit to add your own content.
  - Add and edit more sub-pages until your Portfolio Page is complete.

#### Squarespace Drawbacks
- Squarespace is not cheap. It will cost you approximate $20-$25 per month, compared to $5 per month for normal hosting.
- If you transfer off the platform later, expect to pay to move your domain.
- Dynamic site content isn't really available. If you want to build web apps or interactives, you're not going to be hosting them on squarespace.
- Uniformity. Squarespace/Wordpress template sites all start to look the same.

### Non-Website
- Another popular medium for portfolios is a digital PDF or slide deck. Put everything in a public Google Drive or Dropbox folder. Post hyperlinks in your CV save the entire thing as a .pdf. Beware the size of this option. You can end up with huge .pdf files or videos that are 100mb+. You don't want to send a link to something of that size.

### Tips
- On a recent episode of Data Viz Today podcast, host Alli Torban interviews Danielle Alberti, Data Visualization Editor at Axios. In it, Alberti offers a bunch of really insightful tips about portfolio development (among other things). https://dataviztoday.com/shownotes/74
- Here's another one with Avery Smith, a visualization expert, on why you need to develop skills, a portfolio, and a network to land data jobs. There's a ton of great tips in it about how you can secure your future against the coming tide of AI analysts! https://dataviztoday.com/shownotes/97
- Great piece by Abigail Haddad, a data scientist and ML engineer who used to work with the US government, about how you can develop side projects that will fill your data portfolio and make you more hirable. https://presentofcoding.substack.com/p/make-things-tell-people
- Here are some of my tips. Information overload! Don't have too many pages.
- Show the process, but not too much of it. In other words, dangle a carrot.
- 10-12 projects max, and 3-7 is supposedly the golden rule. Picking 1 or 2 case studies to highlight is a good practice.
- It's OK to show messy work or work-in-progress, but don't get bogged down in the details.
- Have a personality, but avoid featuring a generic one ("hi... i'm xx and i'm a creative interested in..."). There are so many boring landing pages http://lucaorio.com/ with a typical headshot or smiling profile shot on a light background. "I love working with..." If you don't, don't lie! You know who loves those profiles? UX designers!!
- Don't get too political. Avoid things that might throw up flags for a hiring manager. Avoid jargon. "This piece explores the subjective experience of blah blah blah." Know your audience.
- Avoid a ton of mobile app design or redesigns. They look like UXD student projects, not professional work.
- Get feedback from peers.
- Add your cv to your site, not just your LinkedIn profile. Highlight anything collaborative, especially if you have collaborations with external partners.
- Don't try to be a UX designer with your portfolio unless you are one. demonstrate your own data-specific knowledge and previous domain knowledge. If you don't have a history of projects to mine, ask yourself what else you can include. Have you been knitting all pandemic? Have an etsy page/store? Highlight that!! You can even include unsolicited critiques or redesigns of existing projects or interactives.
- Final tip: I expect that you all will ask an AI system to do this for you. "ChatGPT... make an analytics portfolio for me!" Probably not a bad idea, especially if you have a GitHub account and use GitHub Pages to host your portfolio, but be mindful of how AI-y your end result looks. Do you want to signal to your future employer that you offload all of your tasks to AI systems? If not, hand-craft this as much as possible.
- Ok... that is all. It’s hard to get a job without a good portfolio, so put some effort into creating one. Remember, portfolio development is basically curation!
